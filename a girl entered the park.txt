The park had not seen a visitor in three hundred years.

Elara stood at the iron gate, its black bars wound thick with silver vines blooming white even in winter. The sign above had long been swallowed by moss. She pushed the gate open anyway. It did not creak. It had been waiting.

The path inside was cobblestone, worn smooth by centuries of stillness. Lanterns hung from the branches of ancient trees on either side, swaying without wind, glowing the soft blue-white of captured moonlight. Their chains were invisible. They simply floated, as though the trees had grown them like fruit.

The trees themselves were wrong in the most beautiful way. Their trunks rose dark and vast, their canopies a deep violet that shifted to gold where the light touched. Golden leaves drifted down around Elara as she walked, not falling but floating, spiralling slowly as if time moved differently here. She did not try to catch them. She understood somehow that they were not meant to be held.

She walked deeper. The lanterns followed. Not all at once — one would drift from its branch as she passed, then another, until a soft
constellation of warm light surrounded her on all sides. The forest floor glittered with fallen gold. The mist between the trees was not cold but warm, carrying the scent of rain and ancient earth and something sweeter she had no name for.

At the heart of the park she found the fountain.

It was carved from pale stone, wide and still, and the water in it did not move. It held the reflection of the sky above — a sky full of stars that should not have been visible at this hour. Elara looked up. The canopy was open in a perfect circle above the fountain, and through it the stars were sharp and close, as if the park had its own sky entirely separate from the world outside its walls.

She sat on the stone bench beside the fountain.

The fox appeared without sound. It was the colour of embers — deep amber and burnt orange — and its eyes were the same blue-white as the lanterns. It sat beside her on the bench without hesitation, as though it had done this before. As though it had been sitting here waiting for exactly her.

The lanterns drifted in close around them both. The golden leaves swirled once and settled. The fountain caught the starlight and scattered it across the stones like scattered coins.

Elara did not ask the fox what it was or why it had come. She already knew, the way you know things in places like this, without being told.

The park had chosen her. And she had chosen it back the moment she put her hand on that gate and pushed.

She stayed until the stars moved. She would come back tomorrow. And the day after. And every day that followed until the park decided she had learned everything it had to teach.

She was already looking forward to it.
